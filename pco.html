Questions

Q: We'd like to get an understanding of how you see this role. What are your most and least favorite parts of product management? How do you do know if you're doing a good job?

A: As a goal oriented person product management allows me to see physical progress in each facet of the project and helps to engage the social side of my personality in supporting the team. I’m a relational person by nature and the opportunity to invest in people is not only something I genuinely enjoy, but it allows them to work out of their strengths instead of being worried about weakness or fear.

The hardest part of the job is when I want to be helpful and can’t. Sometimes it’s my lack of skill, time, or the greater needs of the project, but it’s always more fun to be part of the solution than bringing up challenges and not being able to help see them through.

I think a job well done is when you have an active team that are excited about solving new problems and are asking challenging questions. Active to me means they're learning, pushing for better solutions, feel freedom to discuss frustrations, and have time to make mistakes. I’ve had jobs that make me feel bored even though I’m busy, and jobs that stress me out even though theres not a mountain of work to do. Management, team communication, and timeline visibility had a lot to do with those factors. If I’m successful those things will be freeing.


Q: What have you found to be effective frameworks/practices for product management?

A: I’ve tried to stay away from latching on to any specific framework in the past. I’ve worked in environments where attempting Waterfall methods in a company that is already on the Agile end of the spectrum creates unnecessary confusion.

What I try to implement are useful tools that allow for clear communication and increased visibility into project milestones. My requirements are only that they increase collaboration and add to the teams ability to get help when it’s needed.

In that vein Trello is a tool I employ on 90% or projects. I like that it easily tracks ideas from the start and allows for things to be revisited easily with clear insight into what might have derailed them. I also try and use Slack as much as possible especially when there are remote team members. But I’m sure you need no introduction.

Tools like Kissmetrics and Intercom keep an eye on where users are entering, where they're spending timed where the biggest bounce rates happen, but those were primarily used for monitoring retention and sales outcomes and helping further design thinking within UX teams. If you have clear channels for direct user feedback it usually trumps the cost necessary to employ tools like this.

As far as design, Invision was a really great tool that I wish I had more experience using. It was utilized at a smaller company I worked with in Austin for a few months and it really helped create feasibility studies for product features without getting deep into development. Id like to see how it’s progressed in the past two years.

At the end of the day I attempt to on listening, ask thoughtful questions, communicating frequently and concisely, and helping where I can. Best practices and useful tools come as a team gets to know each other and learn their collective blind spots.

Q: Products are made by people. What are some of the "tools" in your people management toolbox for fostering and leading healthy teams?

A: I firmly believe that team health means a focus on the group and individuals separately. The group needs to know what the ground rules are. How do we work together, how do we address conflict, how do we argue, and what do we need to be successful. Team citizenship shouldn't be left to chance. Once effective teamwork has been defined, everyone holds everyone accountable, the team looks out for each other.

BWith individuals the responsibility is for clear communication and learning what that means to the other person. We all speak different languages, have different emotional thresholds, backgrounds, fears, and frustrations. None of those should be met with assumptions, nor should situations be avoided if they are at an impasse.

Regular communication as a team in the past has meant 30 minute stand-ups to check on progress and priorities as a group two or three times a week, daily one-on-one checkins to share in victories and incorporate help when its needed.
