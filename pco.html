Q: We'd like to get an understanding of how you see this role. What are your most and least favorite parts of product management? How do you do know if you're doing a good job?

A: I love that product management allows me to see physical progress in each facet of the project while still engaging the social side of my personality. I’m relational by nature, so the opportunity to invest in people is not only something I genuinely enjoy, but it also allows a real relationship to develop. In my experience, this helps a team work out of their strengths instead of being worried about weakness or fear.

The most difficult part of the product management is when I want to be helpful but can’t. Sometimes it’s my lack of skill, time, or the greater needs of the project, but it’s always more fun to be part of the solution than to bring up challenges and be unable to help see them through.

In this kind of situation having an active team that is excited about solving new problems, asks challenging questions, and pushes for better solutions, means that they feel the freedom to discuss frustrations and figure out their mistakes. A product manager is only one factor in the equation (amazing people are 90%), but if if the manager can help multiply that progress and momentum even by a tenth of a percent then I think it's a job well done. I’ve had jobs that make me feel bored even though I’m busy, and jobs that stress me out even though there's not a mountain of work to do. Managing expectations, effective communication, and timeline visibility has a lot to do with those factors. If I’m successful, those on my team will feel free to do their job with confidence.


Q: What have you found to be effective frameworks/practices for product management?

A: I’ve generally stayed away from latching on to any specific framework in the past. I’ve worked in environments where implementing waterfall processes in a company that is already on the agile end of the spectrum created unnecessary confusion.

My goal is to employ useful tools that allow for clear communication and increased visibility into project milestones. My requirements are only that they increase collaboration and increase the team's ability to get help when needed.

In that vein, Trello is a tool I have relied on heavily in the past. I appreciate that it tracks ideas from the start and allows them to be easily revisited. It's also useful for creating a catalogue of possible future changes that are visible enough to continue to modify without having to dig deep into a file structure. I also love Slack, and use it as much as possible. I've found it especially useful when working with remote team members. (But I’m sure you need no introduction.)

Tools like Kissmetrics and Intercom keep an eye on where users are entering, where they're spending time, and where the biggest bounce rates happen. These tools are primarily used for monitoring retention and sales outcomes, as well as furthering design thinking within UX teams. However, if you have clear channels for direct user feedback, it usually trumps the need to employ tools that add so much cost.

As far as design is concerned, Invision is a great tool that I wish I had more experience with. I was exposed to it at a smaller company I worked with in Austin for a few months and it really helped create feasibility studies for product features without getting too deep into development. I'd like to see how it has progressed in the past two years, and I"m definitely eager to grow in my ability to think like a designer!

At the end of the day, my focus is on listening, asking thoughtful questions, communicating frequently and concisely, and helping where I can. Best practices and useful tools come as a team gets to know each other and learns their collective blind spots. Joining an established team like Planning Center is much different than starting with a new team. I would want to learn what is already working and what the primary challenges are and take steps forward from there.

Q: Products are made by people. What are some of the "tools" in your people management toolbox for fostering and leading healthy teams?

A: I firmly believe that fostering team health comes from focusing on the group and individuals separately. The group needs to know what the ground rules are, how we work together, how we address conflict, how we argue, and what we need to do to be successful. When effective teamwork has been defined, teammates hold one another accountable and look out for each other because its a safe place to so.

When working with individuals, the responsibility is to provide clear communication and to learn what that means to the other person. We all have different emotional thresholds, backgrounds, fears, and frustrations. None of those should be met with assumptions, and any conflict or impasse should be worked through with care and patience.

Effective communication allows time to plan and prioritize around the task at hand but more importantly, it allows us to share in victories, help one another, and develop the relationships that are a foundational part of a successful team.
